Earnings Call Transcript

Polaris (PII) Q4 2025 Earnings Call Transcript

By Motley Fool Transcribing – Jan 27, 2026 at 12:25PM EST

Image source: The Motley Fool.

Date

Jan. 27, 2026 at 11 a.m. ET

Call participants

  • Chief Executive Officer — Michael Speetzen
  • Chief Financial Officer — Robert Mack

Takeaways

  • Sales growth -- Adjusted sales increased 9%; North American retail sales, excluding youth, also rose 9% with share gains across Off-Road, Marine, and Snow segments.
  • Tariff impact -- $37 million in new tariffs offset gross profit gains for the quarter, and total annual tariff exposure remains above $200 million, according to management.
  • Debt reduction -- Repaid approximately $530 million in debt during the full year, ending well below covenant thresholds.
  • Inventory position -- Dealer inventory days are under 100, with the healthiest current to non-current mix reported among OEMs; non-current snowmobile inventory reduced by over 40%.
  • Cost savings -- Achieved over $60 million in operational savings for the year, with $25 million in reduced warranty expense driven by improved quality systems.
  • Organic growth guidance -- 2026 organic sales expected to rise 7%-9% excluding Indian Motorcycle, factoring out $300 million prior year sales and adding $400 million tailwind from aligned shipments and retail.
  • Adjusted EPS guidance -- Management projects 2026 adjusted EPS of $1.50-$1.60, including an estimated $0.75-$0.80 benefit from the Indian Motorcycle separation.
  • EBITDA margin outlook -- Adjusted EBITDA margin is expected to expand by 80-120 basis points in 2026, driven by operational efficiency and partial Indian Motorcycle removal.
  • Free cash flow -- $6.5 million in free cash flow generated for the year; Q4 delivered $120 million free cash flow.
  • China exposure reduction -- Lowered China-based material costs to 14%, continuing toward the target of less than 5% by year-end 2027.
  • Product innovation -- New models drove category share gains: RZR XPS, Ranger 500, and Polaris Expedition in Off-Road; Bennington QX and Godfrey Sandpan in Marine.
  • Operating expense -- Operating expenses ex-Indian Motorcycle expected to decrease approximately 4% in 2026.
  • Transition service agreements -- Expect $30 million-$35 million in income in 2026 from transition service agreements supporting Indian Motorcycle’s spin-off.
  • Q1 2026 forecast -- Anticipate sales growth above 10%, but with a $45 million tariff headwind and negative $0.45 in adjusted EPS for the quarter, as Indian Motorcycle remains included for most of Q1.
  • Dividends -- Marked thirtieth consecutive annual dividend increase, underscoring commitment to dividend aristocrat status.

Risks

  • Tariffs reduced adjusted gross margin by $37 million in the quarter, and management acknowledged tariffs as the "most significant challenge" since the pandemic.
  • On-Road retail declined "low double digits" due to a tough comparison against the prior year’s introduction of the Indian Scout motorcycle.
  • Marine retail fell approximately 13%, with ongoing industry headwinds from higher interest rates and macro uncertainty.
  • Mix is expected to be a headwind in 2026, with management stating, "mix is going to be a headwind this year" due to a higher proportion of value and mid-range models and structural gross profit margin differences in marine and snow segments.

Summary

Polaris (PII 2.25%) delivered 9% sales growth and substantial market share gains across all primary segments, despite absorbing significant new tariffs and operational headwinds. The company successfully implemented over $60 million in structural cost savings and meaningfully reduced dealer inventories, creating a solid foundation for 2026 shipment and retail alignment. Management confirmed that the planned separation of Indian Motorcycle will be immediately accretive to adjusted EPS and EBITDA margin, and reiterated firm organic sales and profit growth targets for the coming year given improved supply chain localization and aggressive tariff mitigation initiatives.

  • The company maintained disciplined capital allocation, with notable debt paydown and a strong free cash flow generation profile.
  • Management forecasted "mid-single-digit sales growth mid to high teens EBITDA margin double-digit EPS growth and mid-20s ROIC" as longer-term objectives.
  • Inventory and supply chains are now positioned to nimbly adjust production in response to real-time retail and dealer inventory signals.
  • Marine divestiture rumors were addressed directly, with management stating, "we have zero intention of divesting the Marine business" and highlighting strong brand positioning.
  • Interest rate and macro pressures, particularly in the recreation and on-road segments, may continue to impact retail activity, while utility segments show resilience.
  • "Transition service agreements or TSAs will be put in place to help ensure the smooth separation of Indian Motorcycle into an independent company. Some examples of TSAs that are expected to be in place are for IT systems, supply agreements and freight. These TSAs are in place to neutralize the cost we are incurring within cost of sales and operating expenses to help stand up Indian Motorcycle independently with the majority of the agreements expected to expire in nine to twelve months." Related to the Indian Motorcycle separation are expected to sunset gradually, minimizing disruption.

Industry glossary

  • PG&A: Parts, garments, and accessories – a category of aftermarket sales including vehicle components and rider gear.
  • Adjusted EBITDA margin: Earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortization, adjusted for significant items, expressed as a margin of sales, and used for core operational benchmarking.
  • Transition service agreements (TSAs): Temporary contractual arrangements where one company provides transitional services, such as IT or logistics, to a divested business post-separation.
  • Dealer inventory days: The average time, in days, that vehicles remain in dealer inventories before sale; a lower number reflects leaner, more efficient channel inventory.
  • Absorption benefit: Cost advantage realized when higher production volume absorbs more of a plant's fixed costs, improving margin performance.

Oil States (OIS) Q4 2025 Earnings Call Transcript

By Motley Fool Transcribing – Feb 20, 2026 at 11:11AM EST

Image source: The Motley Fool.

DATE

Friday, February 20, 2026 at 10 a.m. ET

CALL PARTICIPANTS

  • President and Chief Executive Officer — Cynthia B. Taylor
  • Executive Vice President, Chief Financial Officer, and Treasurer — Lloyd A. Hajdik

TAKEAWAYS

  • Consolidated Revenues -- $178 million, up 8% sequentially and year over year, reflecting increased offshore and international business mix.
  • Adjusted Consolidated EBITDA -- $23 million, representing a 9% sequential increase, at the top of management’s prior guidance range.
  • Net Loss -- $117 million, or $2.04 per share, primarily from $112 million in noncash impairments and restructuring charges in the Downhole Technologies segment.
  • Adjusted Net Income -- $8 million, or $0.13 per share, excluding the impact of noncash impairments and restructuring.
  • Cash Flow From Operations -- $50 million for the quarter, up 63% sequentially, enabling retirement of $50 million of outstanding convertible senior notes.
  • Year-End Net Cash Position -- Cash on hand exceeded total debt by $15 million at year-end following debt repayments.
  • Segment Revenue Mix -- Offshore and international markets comprised 77% of revenues, up from 72% in the prior-year period, after exiting underperforming U.S. land-based operations.
  • Offshore Manufactured Products (OMP) Segment -- $123 million in revenues and $25 million in adjusted segment EBITDA with a 20% margin; backlog reached $435 million, the highest since March 2015, and a quarterly book-to-bill of 1.3 times.
  • Completion and Production Services (CPS) Segment -- $23 million in revenues and $7 million in adjusted segment EBITDA, with margins expanded to 32% from 29% sequentially, aided by restructuring.
  • Downhole Technologies Segment -- $32 million in revenues, up 11% sequentially, and adjusted segment EBITDA of $1.3 million; $112 million in noncash impairment charges due to writing down older product technologies and acquired intangibles.
  • Free Cash Flow (Full Year) -- $94 million, up 92% year over year, exceeding full-year guidance.
  • Shares Repurchased -- $17 million in common stock repurchased during the year, equating to approximately 5% of shares outstanding at the start of the year.
  • Credit Facilities -- New four-year credit agreement signed after year-end, providing up to $75 million revolver and $50 million multi-draw term loan, replacing previous asset-based arrangement.
  • 2026 Guidance -- Full-year revenues expected between $680 million and $700 million and EBITDA in the $90 million to $95 million range.
  • First Quarter 2026 Guidance -- Revenues to range $150 million to $155 million, EBITDA expected at $18 million to $19 million.
  • 2026 Cash Flow from Operations -- $60 million to $65 million expected, lower than 2025 due to anticipated working capital build.
  • 2026 CapEx Plans -- $20 million to $25 million targeted for capital expenditures.

RISKS

  • Downhole Technologies segment recorded $112 million in noncash long-lived asset and inventory impairment charges, reflecting abandonment of older product technology and acquisition-date intangibles.
  • Completion and Production Services segment incurred $5 million in restructuring charges during the quarter, with ongoing costs until facilities and equipment are fully exited and monetized.
  • Management acknowledged, "Cash flows from operations are expected to remain strong in 2026, in a range of $60 million to $65 million, down from 2025 due to expected working capital build."
  • Tariff costs on perforating products materially increased mid-2025, with management stating, "We got hit pretty hard in our cost of goods sold because of orders in place that—when the tariff rate went from 25% to 98% about midyear."

SUMMARY

Oil States International (OIS +0.43%) reported significant sequential and year-over-year revenue growth in the quarter, driven by offshore and international operations following the strategic exit of underperforming U.S. land-based businesses. Quarterly cash flows from operations reached a historical high and were used to retire convertible senior notes, resulting in year-end cash exceeding debt by $15 million. The Offshore Manufactured Products segment achieved its highest backlog since March 2015 at $435 million, supported by a 1.3 times book-to-bill ratio and increased military product awards. The company provided full-year 2026 revenue and EBITDA guidance above prior-year levels, outlined first-quarter expectations reflecting normal seasonality, and highlighted a strategic focus on capital allocation and further shareholder returns as facility exits and restructuring charges decrease.

  • Management noted continued growth in differentiated offshore technologies, including first-time deployments of advanced pressure drilling and deep sea mineral riser systems at record water depths.
  • Share repurchases and the new credit facility enhance financial flexibility after significant deleveraging in 2025.
  • Military products contributed roughly 10% of OMP segment revenues, with growth potential in U.S. Navy and Australian defense applications.
  • Geographic expansion is centered on Brazil, Guyana, Southeast Asia (including new manufacturing capacity in Batam, Indonesia), recovering markets in West Africa, and increased targeting of Middle Eastern and international perforating equipment sales.
  • Productivity gains from operational restructuring and technology mix shifts are expected to generate higher margins and cash flow despite projected year-over-year reductions in U.S. land activity and modest near-term headwinds from residual restructuring charges.

INDUSTRY GLOSSARY

  • Book-to-Bill Ratio: The ratio of orders received (bookings) to revenues billed during a specific period, used as a forward indicator of demand and backlog growth.
  • Managed Pressure Drilling (MPD): An advanced drilling technique enabling precise control of wellbore pressure, improving safety and efficiency in challenging offshore environments.
  • Perforating: The process of creating holes in well casing or liners to connect a wellbore with the reservoir, enabling oil or gas flow.
  • Riser System: A conduit system connecting subsea equipment to surface facilities, critical for oil, gas, and mineral extraction from deepwater locations.
  • Convertible Senior Notes: Debt instruments that can be converted by holders into equity and have seniority in capital structure, commonly used for corporate financing and refinancing.
  • Extended Reach Technology: Equipment and tools designed to access reservoirs at greater distances from the wellbore or surface location, often used for complex offshore or remote onshore drilling projects.
  • Impairment Charge: A noncash expense recognized when carrying value of an asset exceeds its recoverable amount, reflecting a reduction in estimated future cash flows or market value for the asset.

We do hope to work together to expand that product offering. The main thing is this is a legacy product we have had for many years.

There are ebbs and flows depending upon defense spending and the investments made by the U.S. government. Cynthia B. Taylor: The great thing is we do have a global base of operations. Cynthia B. Taylor: Southeast Asia—again, that is not just OMP. Cynthia B. Taylor: I think to be in this business, you have to believe in the macro—i.e., the long-term demand for crude oil and natural gas—which I do. I always have. The U.S. land can flex quicker up and down. The reduced activity you have seen today is simply because crude prices have dipped close to or below $60 a barrel. That has put a dampening effect on international and deepwater activity too. I think that is where the “white space” has come in. The different recognition is that we are clearly underinvesting for the long term if you believe in the ultimate demand for crude oil. I think that is recognized.

The second thing I will tell you, there is a long lead time when you talk about particularly deepwater and international activity that I think there is a recognition we have to get going. It is public to follow the offshore rig companies. You see white space on the drilling cycle. You take that as an indicator of weaker activity. We are beginning to see those white space gaps be filled in, and so rig equipment will tend to lead the recovery, and that can be riser systems, MPD systems for us—the things that we offer—as well as the upgrade and service cycle around that equipment. That plays into our more proprietary products. It is a long-winded way of answering a question that I believe we have underinvested. We are going to have to commit capital. With a weaker market in the U.S. and a lot of competition that came into the market, it was very hard to pass those costs on to our customers.

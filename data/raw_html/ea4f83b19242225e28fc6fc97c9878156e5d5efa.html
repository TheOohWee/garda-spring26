KinderCare (KLC) Q4 2025 Earnings Call Transcript

By Motley Fool Transcribing – Mar 17, 2026 at 12:10AM EST

Image source: The Motley Fool.

DATE

Thursday, March 12, 2026 at 5:00 p.m. ET

CALL PARTICIPANTS

  • Chief Executive Officer — Tom Wyatt
  • Chief Financial Officer — Tony Amandi
  • Vice President of Investor Relations — Olivia Kirrer

TAKEAWAYS

  • Revenue -- KinderCare Learning Companies (KLC 0.52%) reported $688 million in the fourth quarter, up 6% year over year, with approximately $45 million attributed to the extra fifty-third week; on a comparable basis, revenue was essentially flat.
  • Adjusted EBITDA -- $68 million in Q4, including around $12 million from the additional week; full-year adjusted EBITDA was $300 million, a year-over-year increase just under 1%.
  • Adjusted EPS -- $0.12 in Q4, up $0.03 from the prior year; $0.70 for the full year, a rise from $0.40 in 2024.
  • Net loss -- $177 million in Q4, driven primarily by a non-cash goodwill impairment; full-year net loss was $113 million, also largely attributable to this impairment.
  • Same-center occupancy -- 64.5% in Q4, representing a 340 basis-point decline from the prior year; annual same-center occupancy decreased 200 basis points to 67.8%.
  • Segment revenue contribution -- KinderCare Learning Companies brand generated 88% of total revenue; Champions contributed 8%, and Crim Schools 4% for the year.
  • Champions -- $60 million in Q4 revenue, up 12% year over year, with double-digit revenue expansion and supported by 128 net new site additions, ending above 1,150 sites.
  • M&A activity -- Acquired center revenue was $6.2 million in Q4 and $14.9 million for the year; twenty-six tuck-in acquisitions contributed about $23.5 million to annual revenue, with $23 million in cash consideration fully funded from $110 million free cash flow.
  • Closures -- Seven centers closed in Q4, totaling nineteen closures for the year, and offsetting revenue growth by about 1%.
  • Full-year revenue -- $2.73 billion including the fifty-third week, representing a 2.6% increase, with same-center revenue at $2.49 billion, up 2.5%.
  • Tuition growth -- Tuition delivered 2% center growth in Q4, and 2.2% for the year, reflecting lower-than-usual subsidy reimbursement rate increases.
  • SG&A to revenue -- 10.7% in Q4, down from the prior year, due to lower IPO-related and normalized public company costs.
  • Interest expense -- Declined significantly compared to the prior year, following debt repayments and repricing actions post-IPO.
  • Net debt to adjusted EBITDA -- Ended the year at 2.6x, at the lower end of the targeted 2.5x-3.0x range.
  • 2026 guidance -- Projected revenue between $2.7 billion and $2.75 billion, adjusted EBITDA of $210 million-$230 million, and adjusted EPS in the $0.10-$0.20 range; guidance assumes tuition will drive roughly 3% revenue growth, fully offset by a 3% decline in same-center occupancy.
  • CapEx & free cash flow -- CapEx expected at approximately 5% of revenue, mostly directed towards growth; free cash flow projected at $35 million-$40 million for the year.
  • First quarter 2026 outlook -- Revenue anticipated between $664 million-$674 million, adjusted EBITDA of $45 million-$48 million, and adjusted EPS near breakeven.
  • Operational initiatives -- Management announced substantial investments in paid search, and changed incentive compensation to focus entirely on profitable enrollment (“FTE”) growth for all employees in the bonus program.
  • Leadership change -- Michael Canavan now focuses solely on the KinderCare Learning Companies brand, shifting from multiple business areas.
  • Portfolio management -- Management is conducting an aggressive center review and may take additional closures beyond the fifteen to twenty already guided, but specifics are not yet determined.
  • Policy & grants -- CFO Tony Amandi said, “we are high-single-digits millions of grant loss by, you know, around seven to nine, actually probably closer to ten, year over year, with most of that being in the first half of the year of our expectations,” indicating a normalization of grant revenue back to pre-COVID levels.

RISKS

  • CFO Tony Amandi stated, “lower occupancy expectation, that is the biggest thing that we are deleveraging, obviously, with that, and that is impacting all the way down to margins,” signaling margin contraction risk driven by declining enrollment.
  • Management projects that a 3% decline in same-center occupancy will fully offset expected 3% tuition-driven revenue growth, providing no net top-line improvement from price actions.
  • Non-cash goodwill impairment of $177 million in Q4, and related $113 million net loss for the year, reflect ongoing exposure to market-based valuation risk.
  • Guidance includes an anticipated $7 million-$10 million year-over-year decline in grant revenue, mostly in the first half, pressuring EBITDA and margins.

SUMMARY

The earnings call delivered detailed, market-relevant disclosures: the 6% reported Q4 revenue gain was entirely due to a fifty-third week, with comparable revenue flat and profitability constrained by lower enrollment and occupancy. Net loss of $177 million for the quarter, and $113 million for the year, resulted from a significant non-cash impairment driven by market valuation, while cash flow, liquidity, and debt metrics remained stable. Management issued 2026 guidance projecting lower adjusted EBITDA and earnings per share, reflecting headwinds from declining occupancy and state grant reductions, despite ongoing tuition increases and disciplined expense control. Strategic focus is now on restoring center-level execution, increasing marketing investment, and aggressively reviewing underperforming locations for potential closure to align the portfolio and cost structure. The company stated that all performance-based compensation now targets measurable profitable enrollment growth, and leadership roles have shifted to ensure direct accountability for turnaround objectives.

  • Wyatt said, “I really believe that when we get our centers back to, and our center directors back to, focusing on introducing KinderCare Learning Companies to more families, being a part of the community in a bigger way, doing the,” underscoring management’s confidence in internal execution levers to address enrollment weakness.
  • CFO Tony Amandi provided, “Our guide at this point, Andrew, at our 3% down on FTEs assumes a curve that is, for the most part, similar to last year,” clarifying expectations for occupancy recovery are currently baked into standard seasonality trends, with upside possible from new initiatives.
  • Management confirmed no anticipated disruption to federal child care block grants for 2026, based on recent legislative action and government reassurances, but expects minor fluctuations among states.
  • Wyatt emphasized, “I am an ex-retailer, and you know that when you have a multi-site business, you are always evaluating all your centers. You are building some, you are closing some, and you are renovating some others. All of that is what we do, and I just came back with a heightened awareness of that and wanted to be sure that we were as current as we could be, and Tony has done a phenomenal job with that,” illustrating the proactive and ongoing portfolio management approach amid persistent market and operational headwinds.

INDUSTRY GLOSSARY

  • Same-center occupancy: The percentage of available seats filled in centers that have been open continuously through the comparison period, used to assess organic growth excluding acquisitions or closures.
  • B2B (Business-to-Business): Company channels involving child care services provided directly to employer partners, including on-site centers and employer-sponsored enrollment benefits.
  • FTE (Full-Time Equivalent): A staffing and enrollment metric representing the workload of an employed person or enrolled child in a full-time schedule, used for performance measurement and incentive purposes.
  • CCDBG (Child Care and Development Block Grant): A federal funding mechanism supporting child care affordability and access for eligible families, referenced in context of state subsidy programs.
  • Tuck-in acquisition: The purchase of smaller centers or operations intended to supplement the existing portfolio for incremental growth.

GE (GE) Q4 2025 Earnings Call Transcript

By Motley Fool Transcribing – Jan 22, 2026 at 12:40PM EST

  • Chief Financial Officer — Rahul Ghai
  • Vice President, Investor Relations — Blaire Shoor

Takeaways

  • Orders -- Up 74% in the fourth quarter, with the Commercial Engines & Services (CES) segment up 76% and Defense & Propulsion Technologies (DPT) up 61%, reflecting sustained demand acceleration.
  • Revenue -- Increased 20% in the quarter, achieving double-digit growth in both CES (up 24%) and DPT (up 13%).
  • Operating profit -- Rose 14% in the quarter to $2.3 billion; full-year company-wide operating profit advanced 25% to $9.1 billion, with CES profit up 26% to $8.9 billion and DPT profit up 22% to $1.3 billion.
  • EPS -- Increased 19% to $1.57 for the quarter, with annual EPS rising 38% to $6.37.
  • Free cash flow -- Climbed 15% in the quarter to $1.8 billion and 24% for the year to $7.7 billion, with conversion over 100% in the quarter and over 110% for the year.
  • Services growth -- CES services revenue up 26% for the year, driven by 24% growth in internal shop visit revenue and 27% LEAP internal shop visit volume increase; spare parts revenue advanced over 25%.
  • Commercial deliveries -- Commercial unit deliveries grew 25% for the year, including LEAP deliveries up 28% to over 1,800 units, a new program record.
  • Defense output -- Defense engine deliveries increased 30% year over year; DPT full-year backlog rose to $21 billion, up nearly $3 billion.
  • Margin trends -- Full-year company margin improved 70 basis points to 21.4%; CES margin up 40 basis points to 26.6%, while DPT margin expanded 110 basis points to 12.3% for the year.
  • Backlog -- Total company backlog reached approximately $190 billion, up nearly $20 billion from the prior year.
  • 2026 guidance -- Projected low double-digit revenue growth, operating profit of $9.85 billion to $10.25 billion, EPS of $7.10 to $7.40 (up nearly 15% at midpoint), and free cash flow of $8 billion to $8.4 billion with maintained high conversion.
  • LEAP internal shop visits -- Anticipated 25% increase in 2026, supported by expansion of MRO network and ongoing investment.
  • Profitability milestones -- CFO Ghai stated, "We expect Leap OE to be profitable in 2026 as per our prior plans."
  • GE9X losses -- "losses on the 9x programs will double year over year." in 2026, per CFO Ghai, following a couple of $100 million in losses for 2025 in line with expectations.
  • Cost and efficiency initiatives -- Material input from priority suppliers grew over 40% year over year, supporting higher output and improved turnaround times for LEAP, CFM56, and GE90 by over 10% in the quarter.
  • Capacity expansion -- Added MTU Dallas as sixth premier MRO partner, with third-party shop visits now representing 15% of LEAP volume; $500 million of over $1 billion planned MRO investment dedicated to LEAP, expected to roughly double LEAP internal capacity.
  • R&D spend -- Maintaining nearly $3 billion annually, focused on improving engine durability, efficiency, and next-generation propulsion, including hybrid electric initiatives and the RISE program.
  • Segment reporting changes -- Aero derivative engines moving from CES to DPT, shifting approximately $1.4 billion revenue and several hundred million dollars of profit; re-segmentation reflected in 2025 segment financials.
  • CFM56 retirements and shop visits -- CFM56 retirements ended 2025 at about 1.5% of the fleet and are expected to remain near 2% for 2026, with shop visits expected in the 2,300-2,400 range through 2028.

Summary

General Electric (GE 1.49%) achieved significant double-digit growth across orders, revenue, operating profit, and free cash flow in its aerospace business, outpacing previous guidance and driven by robust demand in both commercial and defense segments. Management detailed strategic operational advancements, including improved supplier input, accelerated turnaround times, and expanded MRO network capacity, which elevated output and service volume. Organizational changes, such as integrating TNO into CES and updated segment reporting, were implemented to boost lifecycle management and supply chain synergy. GE Aerospace outlined a near-term outlook for continued revenue, profit, and cash flow expansion in 2026, with key product milestones including LEAP original equipment profitability and projected doubling of GE9X program losses, as well as ongoing productivity and margin stabilization efforts. Full-year guidance is supported by a $190 billion backlog and sustained high R&D investment targeting advanced propulsion technologies and aftermarket support.

  • During fiscal Q4 ended Dec. 31, 2025, both segments exceeded double-digit growth rates, with CES equipment deliveries rising 40% and LEAP engine shipments up 49% in the quarter.
  • CFO Ghai confirmed, "if you put all that together, the total company level we expect high teens revenue growth for the company," implying sequential acceleration across both core segments.
  • Material input improvement from suppliers exceeded 40% for 2025 and achieved over 90% commitment level, directly enhancing operational throughput and productivity.
  • A new agreement with AFTIA Aviation increases MRO access to OEM materials for CFM56 engines, strengthening aftermarket support options.
  • Hybrid electric engine architecture completed ground testing, marking a "first-of-its-kind propulsion milestone" and advancing GE Aerospace's transition toward scalable electrification.
  • Backlog was strengthened by recent customer commitments: Riyadh Air (120 LEAP 1A), fly Dubai (60 GE NX), Pegasus Airlines (up to 300 LEAP 1B), and Delta (30 GE NX for Boeing 787s).
  • Corporate cost is forecast at $1.2 billion to $1.3 billion for 2026, attributed to lower interest income, increased AI investments, and larger internal eliminations.
  • LEAP services profitability is expected to improve with increased external channel shop visits and expanded repair catalog, supporting margin resilience even as OE shipments rise.
  • CFM56 shop visits for 2026 are projected at 2,300-2,400 annually through 2028, exceeding previous July guidance, due to lower-than-expected retirement rates and continued operational demand.
  • Management stated there are "I would say on balance, we were with a lot of customers just this past weekend. They want more, and they want it faster without any compromise respect to safety or quality. It's a fair ask and one that the team is committed to delivering on in the new year here." across wide-body and CFM56 aftermarket activity.

Industry glossary

  • LEAP: An advanced high-bypass turbofan engine platform jointly developed by GE Aerospace and Safran for single-aisle aircraft, notably the Airbus A320neo and Boeing 737 MAX.
  • MRO: Maintenance, Repair, and Overhaul network providing engine servicing and support, both internally within GE and through third-party partners.
  • OE: Original Equipment; in this context, sales or shipments of new aircraft engines by GE Aerospace to manufacturers or airlines.
  • Shop visit: A scheduled engine maintenance event where an engine is removed from the aircraft for major service, refurbishment, or repair.
  • GE9X: GE Aerospace's next-generation large turbofan engine designed exclusively for the Boeing 777X long-haul wide-body aircraft.
  • CMR: Change in Maintenance Reserve; a financial charge related to expected future maintenance costs recorded in prior periods.
  • INEX/NINEX: Programs or sales categories referenced for equipment and profit reporting, likely internal GE terminology for specific engine models; context from transcript needed for precise product identification.
  • Flight deck: GE's internal program for operational excellence focused on improving safety, quality, delivery, and cost performance metrics.
  • TNO: Technology and Operations; an organizational unit within GE Aerospace integrating product line, engineering, and supply chain functions to manage the end-to-end engine lifecycle.
  • Book-to-bill: The ratio of new orders ("bookings") received to the amount of products/services billed within the same period, representing demand strength relative to sales output.
  • AD & A: Acronym referenced in the context of working capital and balance sheet management; clarification requires further company-specific disclosure.

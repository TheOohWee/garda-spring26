By Motley Fool Transcribing – Updated Nov 5, 2025 at 4:13PM EST

TAKEAWAYS

Funds From Operations (FFO) -- $302 million, or 46¢ per unit, a 10% increase year over year.

FFO Growth Target -- Management maintained its guidance to deliver "10% plus FFO per unit growth target for 2025."

New Commercial Contracts -- Signed agreements to deliver 4,000 gigawatt hours per year of renewable generation during the quarter.

Commissioned Capacity -- 1,800 megawatts of new projects were brought online in the period.

Major Nuclear Partnership -- In October, Brookfield Renewable (BEP +2.00%) announced a strategic partnership with the US government and Westinghouse (private) involving a minimum $80 billion investment to build new US nuclear reactors with government backing to arrange financing and order placement.

Westinghouse Contribution -- Westinghouse currently services over 50% of the global nuclear fleet, with roughly 85% of its earnings from long-term operating plant services and nuclear fuel contracts.

Hydroelectric Segment FFO -- Delivered $119 million, representing over 20% growth year over year, on strong performance in Canada and Colombia, higher US pricing, and commercial activities.

Wind and Solar FFO -- Generated a combined $177 million, supported by acquisitions in Nayeon, Geronimo Power, and UK offshore wind. This was partially offset by wind asset sales in the US, Spain, and Portugal since the prior year.

Storage and Sustainable Solutions FFO -- $127 million, reflecting growth from Nayawan and Westinghouse, above the prior year.

Liquidity Position -- Maintained $4.7 billion in available liquidity and reaffirmed a BBB+ investment grade rating.

Financings Executed -- Completed $7.7 billion in new financings in the quarter, totaling $38 billion over the past 12 months; $1.1 billion attributed to asset up financings in Q3 alone.

Capital Recycling Activity -- Closed sales and agreements expected to generate $2.8 billion ($900 million net to Brookfield Renewable), including partial sale of North American distributed generation business and other asset disposals.

Asset Recycling Program -- Sold $1.1 billion of assets acquired via Nayeon within a year, demonstrating rapid asset rotation following the acquisition.

Sector Demand Trends -- Accelerated demand from hyperscalers and sovereign computing drove increased commercial contracting and asset utilization across hydro, wind, and solar portfolios.

Battery Storage Developments -- Commissioned a 340 megawatt battery in Australia, now the country's largest operational battery solution, with storage costs down over 50% in the past year.

Microsoft/Google Hydro Contracts -- Signed new 20-year contract with Microsoft (MSFT 0.60%) for hydro supply in PJM, and executed multiple hydro contracts under prior framework agreements with Google (GOOGL 0.41%).

Valuation Arbitrage -- Management stated, "demand and valuations for recently built contracted high quality operating cash generative renewables assets is significantly higher in the private markets than the public markets."

Target Long-Term Returns -- Management reiterated a 12% to 15% long-term total return objective for the business, with nuclear construction targeted "meaningfully above" according to Connor Teskey this range.

INDUSTRY GLOSSARY

FFO (Funds From Operations): A cash flow metric widely used by infrastructure and real asset investors, representing net income plus depreciation and other non-cash expenses, and excluding changes in working capital.

Up Financing: A form of refinancing that increases the amount of debt secured by an asset, typically to extract additional capital after improving cash flows or contractual arrangements.

Safe Harbor: A provision allowing the company to pre-qualify assets or projects for federal tax credits or regulatory treatment under prescribed rules for a future period.

PJM: The regional transmission organization coordinating electricity supply and demand across much of the eastern United States.

AP1000: Westinghouse's flagship nuclear reactor design, often referenced in nuclear buildouts and new project agreements.

Hyperscaler: Large technology companies deploying massive computing infrastructure (e.g., data centers) and requiring scale energy procurement.

To ensure that the 10-year government bond yield remains around 0%, we will conduct purchases of long-term government bonds without setting an upper limit on the necessary amount. Regarding the purchase of assets other than long-term government bonds, the following will apply. For ETFs and J-REITs, we will conduct purchases as needed, with an upper limit on the pace of balance increase equivalent to approximately 12 trillion yen and approximately 180 billion yen per year, respectively. For CPs and corporate bonds, we will conduct purchases with an upper limit of approximately 20 trillion yen in total until the end of March 2022. The Japanese economy shows some signs of weakness due to the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic, but on the whole, it is recovering. The overseas economy is recovering overall, although there are variations by country and region. However, following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, there have been unstable movements in the international financial capital markets, and resource prices such as oil have also risen significantly, necessitating caution regarding future trends. Under these circumstances, exports and industrial production continue to increase, although they are still affected by supply constraints. Moreover, corporate profits and business sentiment continue to improve overall. While there are signs of weakness in some sectors, capital investment is recovering. Looking at the employment and income environment, there are signs of improvement in some areas, but overall it remains weak. Personal consumption has stalled in its recovery due to increased downward pressure on service consumption from the resurgence of the pandemic. Housing investment is moving within a flat range. Public investment remains at a high level but is showing weaker movements. Japan's financial environment remains accommodative overall, although some tightness remains in corporate financing. In terms of prices, the year-on-year consumer price index (excluding fresh food) is in the mid-0% range, reflecting the impact of lower mobile phone communication fees and rising energy prices. Additionally, the expected inflation rate is gradually rising. Looking ahead at the Japanese economy, it is expected to recover, supported by an increase in external demand, an accommodative financial environment, and the effects of government economic measures, as the downward pressure on service consumption and the impact of supply constraints from COVID-19 ease, despite the influence of rising resource prices. The year-on-year consumer price index (excluding fresh food) is expected to clearly expand its positive range in the near term, as energy prices rise significantly and the pass-through of rising raw material costs progresses, while the impact of falling mobile phone communication fees diminishes. During this period, it is believed that the underlying inflationary pressures will increase against the backdrop of improvements in the macroeconomic supply-demand gap and rising medium- to long-term expected inflation rates. As risk factors, it is necessary to continue to pay attention to the trends of infections, including variants, and their impact on domestic and foreign economies. Additionally, the situation in Ukraine poses a high degree of uncertainty regarding its impact on Japan's economy and prices through international financial capital markets, resource prices, and trends in overseas economies. We will continue to support corporate financing and maintain stability in financial markets through (1) the special program for COVID-19 response funding support, (2) ample supply of yen and foreign currency without setting upper limits through government bond purchases and dollar operations, and (3) purchases of ETFs and J-REITs with an upper limit on the annual increase pace of approximately 12 trillion yen and approximately 180 billion yen, respectively. In the near term, we will closely monitor the impact of COVID-19 and, if necessary, will not hesitate to implement additional monetary easing measures. Regarding the policy interest rate, we assume that it will remain at the current level of short- and long-term interest rates or below. In favor: Members Kuroda, Amamiya, Wakabayashi, Suzuki, Adachi, Nakamura, Noguchi, Nakagawa. Opposed: Member Kataoka. Member Kataoka opposed the decision, arguing that it would be desirable to strengthen monetary easing by lowering short- and long-term interest rates to support positive corporate capital investment in the post-COVID era.

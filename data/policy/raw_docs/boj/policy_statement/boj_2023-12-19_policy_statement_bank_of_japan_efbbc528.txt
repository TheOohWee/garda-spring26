The monetary market adjustment policy until the next monetary policy meeting will be as follows (unanimous agreement). Short-term interest rate: A negative interest rate of -0.1% will be applied. Long-term interest rate: To ensure that the 10-year government bond yield remains around 0%, necessary amounts of long-term government bonds will be purchased without setting an upper limit. The upper limit for long-term interest rates will be aimed at 1.0%, and to promote the formation of a yield curve consistent with the above monetary market adjustment policy, large-scale government bond purchases will continue, and in each maturity, flexible increases in purchase amounts, fixed-rate operations, and common collateral fund supply operations will be implemented. Regarding the purchase of assets other than long-term government bonds, the following will apply. For ETFs and J-REITs, purchases will be made as needed, with an upper limit on the pace of balance increase of approximately 12 trillion yen per year and approximately 180 billion yen per year, respectively. Commercial paper (CP) will maintain a balance of approximately 2 trillion yen. Corporate bonds will be purchased at a pace similar to that before the pandemic, gradually returning the purchase balance to the level before the pandemic (approximately 3 trillion yen). However, adjustments to the purchase balance of corporate bonds will be made with sufficient consideration of the issuance environment for corporate bonds. The economy of our country is gradually recovering. The overseas economy is experiencing a slowdown in its recovery pace. Despite these influences, exports and industrial production are moving within a flat range, supported by the easing of supply constraints. Corporate profits and business sentiment are improving. Under these circumstances, capital investment is showing a gradual increasing trend. The employment and income environment is gradually improving. Personal consumption continues to gradually increase, despite the impact of rising prices. Housing investment is showing weak movements. Public investment is moving within a flat range. The financial environment in our country is in a relaxed state. In terms of prices, the year-on-year change in consumer prices (excluding fresh food) has narrowed compared to earlier due to the downward pressure on energy prices from government economic measures, but is currently around 3% due to the impact of price pass-through stemming from previous increases in import prices. The expected inflation rate is gradually rising. Looking ahead at the economy of our country, it is expected to continue a gradual recovery, supported by the emergence of pent-up demand, although it will face downward pressure from the slowdown in the recovery pace of the overseas economy. Thereafter, it is believed that growth will continue to exceed the potential growth rate as the positive circulation mechanism from income to expenditure gradually strengthens. The year-on-year change in consumer prices (excluding fresh food) is expected to remain above 2% through the next fiscal year due to the lingering effects of price pass-through stemming from previous increases in import prices. Thereafter, it is expected that the year-on-year positive change will narrow as these effects dissipate. During this period, the fundamental inflation rate of consumer prices is expected to gradually rise towards the 'price stability target' as the macroeconomic supply-demand gap turns positive and medium- to long-term expected inflation rates and wage growth rates also increase. Looking at risk factors, uncertainties surrounding the economy and prices in our country are extremely high, influenced by overseas economic and price trends, resource price movements, and corporate wage and price-setting behaviors. Under these circumstances, it is necessary to closely monitor the trends in financial and foreign exchange markets and their impact on our country's economy and prices. In order to achieve the 'price stability target' and to sustain it stably until the necessary point, 'quantitative and qualitative monetary easing with yield curve control' will be continued. Regarding the monetary base, the expansion policy will continue until the year-on-year increase in the consumer price index (excluding fresh food) stabilizes above 2%. Efforts will continue to maintain the funding of businesses and the stability of financial markets, and if necessary, additional monetary easing measures will be taken without hesitation. The yield of fixed-rate operations will be determined as appropriate based on actual interest rates.

The monetary market adjustment policy until the next monetary policy meeting is as follows. Short-term interest rate: A negative interest rate of -0.1% will be applied. Long-term interest rate: To ensure that the 10-year government bond yield remains around 0%, purchases of long-term government bonds will be made without setting an upper limit. The fluctuation range of long-term interest rates will be set at approximately ±0.5%, and limit operations at a yield of 0.5% for 10-year government bonds will be conducted every business day, except in cases where bids are clearly not expected. To promote the formation of a yield curve consistent with the above monetary market adjustment policy, large-scale government bond purchases will continue, and flexibly increase purchase amounts and limit operations will be implemented for each maturity. Regarding the purchase of assets other than long-term government bonds, the following will apply. For ETFs and J-REITs, purchases will be made as needed, with an upper limit of approximately 12 trillion yen and approximately 180 billion yen in annual balance increase pace, respectively. Commercial paper and others will maintain a balance of approximately 2 trillion yen. Corporate bonds and others will be purchased at a pace similar to that before the spread of the infection, gradually returning the purchase balance to the level before the spread of the infection (approximately 3 trillion yen). However, adjustments to the purchase balance of corporate bonds and others will be made with sufficient consideration of the issuance environment for corporate bonds. The economy of our country is recovering, although it is affected by the past high resource prices. The overseas economy is experiencing a slowdown in its recovery pace. Despite these influences, exports and industrial production are moving within a flat range, supported by the easing of supply constraints. Corporate profits are generally at a high level, and capital investment is gradually increasing. The employment and income environment is gradually improving. Personal consumption is gradually increasing, although it is affected by rising prices. Housing investment is showing weak movements. Public investment is gradually increasing. The financial environment in our country is generally relaxed, although some tightness remains in corporate financing. In terms of prices, the year-on-year change in consumer prices (excluding fresh food) has narrowed compared to earlier due to the downward pressure on energy prices from government economic measures, but due to the impact of price pass-through starting from rising import prices, it is currently in the mid-3% range. The expected inflation rate has been flat after rising. Looking ahead at the economy of our country, it is expected to recover gradually, supported by the emergence of pent-up demand, although it will face downward pressure from past high resource prices and the slowdown in the recovery pace of the overseas economy until around the middle of this fiscal year. After that, it is expected to continue growing above the potential growth rate as the positive circulation mechanism from income to expenditure gradually strengthens. However, the growth pace is likely to gradually slow down. The year-on-year change in consumer prices (excluding fresh food) is expected to narrow as the impact of price pass-through starting from rising import prices diminishes by the middle of this fiscal year. After that, as the macroeconomic supply-demand gap improves and changes in corporate pricing and wage-setting behavior occur, the long-term expected inflation rate and wage growth rate are expected to rise, gradually expanding the positive range again with fluctuations. Looking at risk factors, uncertainties surrounding our economy are extremely high due to overseas economic and price trends, developments in the situation in Ukraine, and trends in resource prices. In this context, it is necessary to closely monitor the trends in financial and foreign exchange markets and their impact on our economy and prices. In order to achieve the 'price stability target' and to sustain it stably until the necessary point, 'quantitative and qualitative monetary easing with yield curve control' will be continued. Regarding the monetary base, the expansion policy will continue until the year-on-year increase in the consumer price index (excluding fresh food) stabilizes above 2%. Efforts will continue to maintain the funding of companies and the stability of financial markets, and if necessary, additional monetary easing measures will be taken without hesitation.

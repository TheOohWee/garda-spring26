Earlier today the Federal Open Market Committee announced a ½ percentage point reduction in the target range for the federal funds rate, bringing that range to 1 to 1¼ percent. My colleagues and I took this action to help the U.S. economy keep strong in the face of new risks to the economic outlook. The fundamentals of the U.S. economy remain strong. The unemployment rate has been near half-century lows for well more than a year, the pace of job gains has been solid, and wages have been rising. Since then, the spread of the coronavirus has brought new challenges and risks. The magnitude and persistence of the overall effects on the economy, however, remain highly uncertain, and the situation remains a fluid one. In response, we have eased the stance of monetary policy to provide some more support to the economy. In the weeks and months ahead, we will continue to closely monitor developments and their implications for the economic outlook, and we will use our tools and act as appropriate to support the economy. We’ve seen a broader spread of the virus—we’ve seen it begin to spread a bit here in the United States. I fully expect it will return to solid growth and a solid labor market as well. We’re in active discussions with central banks around the world on an ongoing basis. We have a full plate with monetary policy. We do hear concerns, particularly from those most directly exposed, but there’s nothing in that nature: Financial markets are functioning in an orderly manner. We do believe that our action will provide a meaningful boost to the economy. We’re always going to set monetary policy at a given time in a way that we think best serves our dual-mandate goals. We will not do that, and it’s very important that the public understand that.

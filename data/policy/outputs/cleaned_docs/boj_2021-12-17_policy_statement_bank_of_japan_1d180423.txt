The proper loans for small and medium-sized enterprises related to the response to the infection will be extended for six months under the current handling. The institutional loans for small and medium-sized enterprises related to the response to the infection will have the interest rate for the loan promotion system set at 0% (Category III) from April 2022, and the inclusion in the macro addition balance will be based on the equivalent amount of the utilized balance, with the deadline extended for six months as a back-financing measure. The private debt collateral for large enterprises and housing loans will end as scheduled at the end of March 2022. The increase in the purchase of CP and corporate bonds will end as scheduled at the end of March 2022. From April 2022, the purchase pace will return to the level before the spread of the infection, gradually reducing the balance of CP and corporate bonds to the level before the spread of the infection (approximately 2 trillion yen for CP, approximately 3 trillion yen for corporate bonds). To ensure that the 10-year government bond yield remains around 0%, necessary amounts of long-term government bonds will be purchased without setting an upper limit. Regarding ETFs and J-REITs, purchases will be made as necessary, with an upper limit on the balance increase pace of approximately 12 trillion yen per year for ETFs and approximately 1.8 billion yen per year for J-REITs. For CP and corporate bonds, purchases will be made with a total balance limit of approximately 20 trillion yen until the end of March 2022. The economy of our country continues to be in a severe state due to the impact of the novel coronavirus infection, but is recovering on a fundamental basis. The overseas economy is recovering overall, although there are variations by country and region. Although exports and industrial production are still showing weak movements due to supply constraints, they continue to increase on a fundamental basis. Corporate profits and business sentiment continue to improve overall. Capital investment is recovering, although some sectors are showing weakness. Looking at the employment and income environment, weak movements continue due to the impact of the infection. Personal consumption is gradually recovering as the downward pressure on service consumption due to the infection has somewhat eased. Housing investment is recovering. Public investment is at a high level but is showing weaker movements. The financial environment in our country is overall relaxed, although some tightness remains in corporate financing. In terms of prices, the year-on-year change in consumer prices (excluding fresh food) shows the impact of reductions in mobile phone communication fees, while energy prices are rising, resulting in a level of around 0%. In addition, the expected inflation rate is recovering. Looking ahead at the economy of our country, it is expected to recover with the easing of downward pressure on service consumption and the impact of supply constraints, supported by an increase in external demand, a relaxed financial environment, and the effects of government economic measures. After that, as the positive circulation mechanism from income to expenditure strengthens throughout the economy, including the household sector, the economy of our country is expected to continue to grow above its potential growth rate, albeit at a slower pace. The year-on-year change in consumer prices (excluding fresh food) is expected to gradually expand its positive range in the near term, reflecting the rise in energy prices. After that, while accompanied by fluctuations due to temporary factors, it is believed that the trend will gradually increase the rate of inflation against the backdrop of improvements in the macroeconomic supply-demand gap and rising long-term expected inflation rates. As risk factors, it is necessary to continue to pay attention to the trends of the infection and its impact on the domestic and foreign economies. In particular, there is a high level of uncertainty about whether the balance between infection control and economic activity will proceed smoothly, and it is also necessary to be aware of the risk that the impact of supply constraints observed in some areas may expand and prolong. Continuing with (1) the special program for funding support related to COVID-19, (2) abundant supply of yen and foreign currency without setting upper limits through government bond purchases and dollar operations, and (3) purchases of ETFs and J-REITs under the upper limit of approximately 12 trillion yen and approximately 1.8 billion yen annual increase pace, efforts will be made to support corporate financing and maintain financial market stability. In the near term, we will closely monitor the impact of the novel coronavirus infection and, if necessary, will not hesitate to take additional monetary easing measures. Regarding the policy interest rate, it is assumed that it will remain at the current level of short- and long-term interest rates or below. In favor: Members Kuroda, Amemiya, Wakabayashi, Suzuki, Adachi, Nakamura, Noguchi, Nakagawa. Opposed: Member Kataoka. Member Kataoka opposed the decision, stating that it would be desirable to strengthen monetary easing by lowering short- and long-term interest rates to support positive capital investment by companies in the post-COVID era.

The monetary market adjustment policy until the next monetary policy decision meeting will be as follows (unanimous agreement). Short-term interest rate: A negative interest rate of -0.1% will be applied. Long-term interest rate: To ensure that the 10-year government bond yield remains around 0%, purchases of long-term government bonds will be made without setting an upper limit. The fluctuation range of long-term interest rates will be aimed at around ±0.5%, and the operation of the yield curve control will be conducted more flexibly. For the 10-year government bond yield, limit order operations at a yield of 1.0% will be conducted every business day, except in cases where bids are clearly not expected. To promote the formation of a yield curve consistent with the above monetary market adjustment policy, large-scale government bond purchases will continue, and flexibly increase purchase amounts, limit orders, and common collateral funding operations will be implemented for each maturity. Regarding the purchase of assets other than long-term government bonds, the following will apply. For ETFs and J-REITs, purchases will be made as needed, with an upper limit of approximately 12 trillion yen and approximately 180 billion yen per year for balance increase pace, respectively. Commercial paper and others will maintain a balance of approximately 2 trillion yen. Corporate bonds and others will be purchased at a pace similar to that before the spread of the infection, gradually returning the purchase balance to the level before the spread of the infection (approximately 3 trillion yen). However, adjustments to the purchase balance of corporate bonds will be made with sufficient consideration of the issuance environment for corporate bonds. The economy of our country is gradually recovering. The overseas economy is experiencing a slowdown in its recovery pace. Despite these influences, exports and industrial production are moving within a flat range, supported by the easing of supply constraints. Corporate profits are generally at a high level, and capital investment is gradually increasing. The employment and income environment is gradually improving. Personal consumption is steadily increasing at a gradual pace, despite the impact of rising prices. Housing investment is showing weak movements. Public investment is gradually increasing. The financial environment in our country is in a relaxed state. In terms of prices, the year-on-year consumer price index (excluding fresh food) has narrowed its positive range compared to earlier due to the downward pressure on energy prices from government economic measures, but is currently around 3% due to the impact of price pass-through stemming from previous increases in import prices. The expected inflation rate is showing signs of rising again. Looking ahead at the economy of our country, it is expected to continue a gradual recovery, supported by the emergence of pent-up demand, although it will face downward pressure from the slowdown in the recovery pace of the overseas economy. Thereafter, it is believed that growth will continue to exceed the potential growth rate as the positive circulation mechanism from income to expenditure gradually strengthens. The year-on-year consumer price index (excluding fresh food) is expected to narrow its positive range after the impact of price pass-through stemming from previous increases in import prices diminishes, and as the macroeconomic supply-demand gap improves, it is anticipated that the long-term expected inflation rate and wage growth rate will also rise, gradually expanding the positive range again. Looking at risk factors, uncertainties surrounding the economy and prices in our country are extremely high, influenced by overseas economic and price trends, resource price movements, and corporate wage and price-setting behavior. In this context, it is necessary to closely monitor the trends in financial and foreign exchange markets and their impact on our country's economy and prices. In order to achieve the 'price stability target' and to sustain it stably until the necessary point, 'quantitative and qualitative monetary easing with yield curve control' will be continued. Regarding the monetary base, the expansion policy will continue until the year-on-year increase rate of the consumer price index (excluding fresh food) stabilizes above 2%. Efforts will continue to maintain the funding of businesses and the stability of financial markets, and if necessary, additional monetary easing measures will be taken without hesitation. Under the current interest rate situation, bids are not expected, but operations will be conducted every business day for the time being.
